Key Findings

Four Critical Observations From The Session

The clearest findings: strong general strength, very good coachability, a right patellar tendon issue that is currently manageable but important, flat/under-organized feet that respond well to cues, left-side balance and foot-control challenges, tight hips, excellent upper-body/core strength, and better running mechanics as pace increased.

You moved well through the warm-up diagnostic. Your bodyweight squats showed good form once cued. Your push-ups showed excellent core and plank organization. Your A-skips were excellent, and your wicket A-skips became very good after you absorbed the cues.

This matters because you do not need a full mechanical rebuild. You already have the baseline strength and coordination to become a better runner. The main job is to reorganize some movement patterns so they are more useful for running: more foot tripod, more single-leg stability, more hip/ankle control, and less reliance on the knee as the default stabilizer.

Your best movement quality appeared when you slowed down, rooted the foot, pushed into the ground, stayed tall, and let the hip/glute system organize the leg. When you rushed, balance became more challenging. When you paused and created structure first, the movement improved quickly.

You came into the session with a history of right patellar tendon swelling after a direct knee impact. You reported thickening of the tendon and some fluid from imaging, and you noted that deeper knee-bend positions, front squats, basketball, faster running, and longer-than-normal runs can provoke symptoms afterward.

During the assessment, pain stayed low. Baseline acute pain was 0/10. However, the right knee showed up subtly during Cossack squats, squat jumps, depth jumps, and treadmill running. The pattern was not severe, but it was consistent enough to matter.

This is not a reason to avoid running. It is a reason to build intelligently. You can train through low-level tendon awareness if symptoms stay controlled, but the right knee should not be pushed past the "useful feedback" zone. Pain above 3/10, pain that increases during the run, or pain that is worse the next morning should trigger a training adjustment.

Single-leg work revealed the most important mechanical opportunity. During RDLs, balance and coordination were more challenging, and the left side felt more challenged. During rotational RDLs, the right planted leg looked more stable than the left, while the bottom of the left foot felt a little crampy. That is useful information.

The left foot/arch system appears to need more specific strength and coordination. Your feet can create structure when cued, but they do not automatically live there yet. This matters because running is repeated single-leg loading. If the foot and ankle do not organize quickly, the knee, hip, or trunk has to do more emergency stabilization.

The goal is not to force a perfect arch or make your foot rigid. The goal is to create a functional tripod: big toe, little toe, heel. From there, the ankle can adapt, the knee can track cleaner, and the hip/glute system can contribute more effectively.

Your treadmill mechanics improved at faster speeds. In the Adidas Boston 13, the fast reps progressed from approximately 4:39/km to 4:18/km to 4:09/km by Wahoo lap average. Your cadence increased from roughly 175 spm to 181 spm to 186 spm, and your stride looked better as rhythm improved.

In the ON Cloudflyer, the faster reps progressed from approximately 4:40/km to 4:09/km to 4:02/km by Wahoo lap average, with pain staying at 0/10 during those fast reps. This is encouraging because it shows that your body can access better rhythm and quicker turnover.

The caution is that faster running feeling smoother does not mean it should dominate training. Given your right patellar tendon history, current low running frequency, and goal of building toward half marathon durability, faster work should be used as a technical tool: short, controlled, fully recovered, and stopped before mechanics or symptoms deteriorate.

Biomechanical Analysis

Footstrike, Asymmetry & Vertical Mechanics

Footstrike Pattern

Lands with a heel-to-midfoot pattern, especially at easier and controlled paces. This is not automatically a problem — forcing a forefoot strike would likely increase calf, Achilles, plantar fascia, and shin demand too quickly, which would not be ideal while managing patellar tendon symptoms.

The goal is not to change footstrike artificially, but to improve where the foot lands relative to the body, reduce braking, keep contact quiet, and allow the foot to roll forward smoothly. At faster paces, cadence increased and the stride looked cleaner — mechanics respond well to rhythm.

Foot And Arch Pattern

Feet are trainable. Flat or under-organized foot posture at baseline, but arches appeared when cued into foot tripod, external organization, and tall posture — a very good sign.

During single-leg work, the left foot and left-side balance were more challenged; during rotational RDLs the left foot became crampy on the bottom, likely reflecting the foot intrinsics working harder than usual. Corrective goal: foot tripod, short-foot control, tibialis strength, soleus endurance, calf capacity, and ankle problem-solving before the knee gets involved.

Knee, Hip & Trunk Pattern

The right knee is the symptom site and should be monitored closely. The patellar tendon became noticeable in deeper knee flexion positions, especially Cossack squats, squat jumps, and depth jumps. The treadmill did not provoke major pain, but there was mild right knee awareness and a slight hitch observed around threshold.

Hips are tight, especially through hip flexion and lateral hip/TFL positions, with a significant old left hip flexor injury that may still influence pelvis/hip organization. Trunk strength is a major positive — push-ups and plank structure were strong, and bar hang held 62 seconds with grip as the limiter.

Return To Running Progression

Build toward 2–3 easy runs per week. Keep them short, controlled, and repeatable. The purpose is to teach the tendon that running is normal.

Once the knee is quiet, add 4–6 short strides after an easy run. Keep them relaxed and smooth — technical, not a workout.

Add controlled intervals around 5:10–5:25/km. Supports 8K and 10K goals without forcing race effort too often.

Build the long run gradually for the Toronto Half Marathon. Don't rush — the sub-2 goal requires durability more than speed.

Once the knee tolerates consistent volume, add longer controlled work around half marathon goal pace. Should feel calm, not like a time trial.

Progression Rule: Each stage must be tolerated before progressing — symptoms stay low during the run, do not increase after stopping, and are not worse the next morning.

Red Flags For Coaches

Pain After Stopping

Pain that builds after the session matters even if pain was low during the run.

Next-Day Tendon Irritation

If the right tendon feels swollen, stiff, sharp, or worse the next morning, reduce the next session.

Knee Pain Above 3/10

If knee pain reaches 3/10 during a run, stop the quality portion and return to easy running or walking.

Limping Or Protective Mechanics

If a hitch, limp, altered stride, or protective loading is noticed, stop the fast work.

Repeated Rib Cramping

If this repeats, review breathing, fueling, hydration, warm-up intensity, and whether effort is too high too soon.
